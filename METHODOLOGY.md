# Analytical Methodology

**NormTrace-IHR (IHR 2005+), Version 1.0.0**

Santos-Dominguez, A.B. (2025)

---

## The analytical question

NormTrace-IHR is designed to answer a specific and narrow question: does a State Party's domestic legal order contain the instruments that would be needed to exercise the powers that IHR compliance requires? This is distinct from asking whether the country has the technical capacity to conduct surveillance, whether its laboratories can confirm a pathogen, or whether its health workforce is adequately trained. Those questions are answered by the Joint External Evaluation (JEE) and related capacity assessment instruments. The question here is prior to all of those: if a minister of health needed to compel a traveller to undergo quarantine, or an authority at an airport needed to detain a vessel, or a national focal point needed to share identifiable health data with the WHO within 24 hours -- does the domestic legal order give them the power to do so?

The distinction matters because capacity and legal authority are not interchangeable. A government can have excellent epidemiological surveillance infrastructure and no legal mandate to notify the WHO within the timeframes the IHR requires. It can have a well-resourced biosafety laboratory and no statutory basis for restricting the entry of contaminated cargo. It can have experienced public health officials and no legal instrument coordinating their actions with the immigration authority at points of entry. When those situations arise -- typically in the first hours of a public health emergency of international concern -- the absence of a legal basis does not merely create procedural inconvenience. It creates vulnerability to judicial challenge, institutional paralysis, and ultimately to the kind of fragmented response that the IHR was designed to prevent.

## Why legal instruments specifically, and not policy or administrative practice

The conventional public administration literature distinguishes between norms (legally binding instruments that create obligations enforceable through legal processes) and policies (expressions of institutional intent that create expectations but not enforceable obligations). This distinction is foundational to administrative law in both civil law and common law traditions, though it is expressed through different doctrines.

In civil law systems -- which govern the majority of IHR States Parties, including the full Latin American region, continental Europe, most of Francophone Africa, and significant portions of Asia -- the principle is known as the *principe de legalite* in French tradition, *Gesetzmassigkeit der Verwaltung* in German-Austrian tradition, and *principio de legalidad* in Spanish and Latin American tradition. The principle holds that any act of public authority that restricts the rights of private persons must be authorised by a legal norm of appropriate rank. An administrative circular, a ministerial protocol, or an institutional plan may express the government's intentions, but they do not confer the power to apply coercive measures against an unwilling subject. That power comes only from a norm in the formal legal hierarchy.

In common law systems, the equivalent doctrine is expressed as the rule of law requirement that public authority must have a legal basis -- that the exercise of power without statutory foundation is an excess of jurisdiction susceptible to judicial review. The specific doctrinal formulations differ between Westminster-derived systems, but the functional consequence is the same: a public official cannot lawfully quarantine a traveller, seize a conveyance, or compel disclosure of personal health information without a legal authority for that action.

The *reserve of law* doctrine -- *reserve de loi*, *Vorbehalt des Gesetzes*, *reserva de ley* -- adds a further constraint in many systems: certain categories of public action are constitutionally reserved to the legislature precisely because they are too consequential to be delegated to executive discretion. The deprivation of liberty (including quarantine and isolation), the compulsory processing of personal health data, and the imposition of obligations on private enterprises are typically in this category. A ministerial regulation that purports to authorise these actions without an enabling statute is constitutionally defective in many jurisdictions, regardless of how clearly it expresses the minister's intent.

Policy instruments are also more fragile than norms from a durability perspective. A government policy can be revoked by the same minister who issued it, without legislative process, often without publication or public notice. A statutory provision, once enacted, requires legislative action to repeal. This has practical consequences for preparedness: a country whose IHR capacity depends on a ministerial circular has a legal framework that can evaporate between elections. A country whose IHR capacity rests on enacted legislation has a more durable foundation.

For all these reasons, NormTrace-IHR restricts its analysis to binding normative instruments and does not treat policy, protocol, guideline, or administrative practice as sufficient basis for IHR powers that require coercive authority.

## The criterion for selecting IHR provisions

The IHR contains 66 articles and 9 Annexes. Not all of them require an express domestic legal basis. Some provisions address the WHO's own obligations (Arts. 11, 12, and the institutional provisions of Parts IX and X). Others describe best practices or recommendations that States may follow without any specific domestic enabling act. Still others require only that the State refrain from taking certain actions -- prohibitions that in most legal systems apply without needing a positive enabling norm.

The provisions selected for analysis in NormTrace-IHR are those that require an act of positive public authority affecting the rights or obligations of private persons or creating binding coordination across institutional hierarchies. The selection criterion applies six tests:

**Test 1.** Does the provision authorise or require the State to apply a coercive measure affecting the rights of private persons? Coercive measures include quarantine, isolation, compulsory medical examination, denial of entry, detention of conveyances, mandatory disclosure of information, and destruction or decontamination of goods. If yes, a statutory basis is required.

**Test 2.** Does the provision require the designation of a specific institutional actor with a defined mandate? If yes, a formal act of designation -- at minimum a published administrative act, and in many systems a statutory provision -- is required. A general ministerial mandate is insufficient where the IHR specifies a discrete function.

**Test 3.** Does the provision require the State to share, restrict access to, or process personal health data? If yes, an express basis is required in data protection legislation, which typically requires statutory exception clauses for public health purposes.

**Test 4.** Does the provision impose obligations on private parties -- carriers, operators, healthcare providers, laboratories? If yes, a domestic transposition norm is required, because private parties are not directly bound by international treaty obligations.

**Test 5.** Does the provision require intersectoral coordination across institutions operating under distinct legal mandates? If yes, a protocol or memorandum of understanding is insufficient where the institutions have conflicting statutory mandates or parallel hierarchies. A legal instrument establishing the coordination structure and specifying which authority prevails in cases of conflict is required.

**Test 6.** Can the provision be satisfied by the executive's ordinary powers -- internal planning, information-sharing between government departments, technical capacity-building? If yes, and none of the preceding tests apply, the provision is resolvable by administrative action and is excluded from the normative analysis.

Two IHR provisions are especially explicit about their requirement for domestic legislative action. Article 31.2 provides that States Parties may compel travellers to undergo medical examination or health measures "in accordance with its national legislation" -- a formulation that makes domestic law a precondition of the power's existence, not merely a procedural constraint on its exercise. Article 4.2bis, introduced by the 2024 amendments, requires that States Parties take measures to implement the institutional obligation of the article "including, as appropriate, adjusting their domestic legislative and/or administrative arrangements." These two provisions serve as interpretive anchors for the application of the criterion across the rest of the instrument.

Applying these tests across all 66 articles and 9 Annexes produces a set of 29 provisions grouped in seven analytical blocks (A through G), covering institutional architecture, core surveillance and response capacities, points of entry, measures on persons and goods, data and documentary requirements, additional measures and accountability, and an inverse compatibility check.

The inverse compatibility block (G) differs from the others: rather than identifying gaps where domestic law does not provide what the IHR requires, it verifies that existing domestic law does not contradict prohibitions or limitations that the IHR imposes on State action -- for example, that domestic law does not routinely impose measures on transit conveyances that the IHR restricts to situations of demonstrated risk.

## The four-link enablement chain

For each selected provision, the analysis applies a structured four-link test to the instruments in the validated corpus. All four links must be present for a provision to be considered normatively enabled.

**Norm.** A legally binding instrument of appropriate rank exists and is applicable to the subject matter in question. A protocol is not a norm. A guideline is not a norm. A norm in the present sense is an instrument that creates binding obligations enforceable through legal processes -- a statute, an executive regulation issued under statutory authority, a mandatory technical standard (where the enabling statute provides for such standards to have binding force), or a published administrative act with general and external binding effect.

**Actor.** A specific institutional actor is designated with responsibility for this function. Diffuse responsibility across a ministry or across the health system generally is not equivalent to designation. Where the IHR requires a "competent authority" with specific functions at a point of entry, or a "national IHR focal point" accessible at all times, the designation of that actor must be traceable to a published legal or administrative instrument.

**Authority.** The designated actor is conferred an explicit power to take the required action. Implied powers -- powers inferred from a general mandate without specific enumeration -- are legally weaker and contestable. They are treated in this analysis as partial rather than full compliance. Where the action involves restriction of private rights (quarantine, denial of entry, compulsory examination), explicit rather than implied authority is required.

**Enforceability.** A mechanism exists to compel compliance and to hold relevant actors accountable. This has two dimensions: external enforceability (a mechanism to compel compliance by regulated private parties, such as carriers or healthcare providers) and internal accountability (a mechanism to require and verify that the designated public authority actually exercises its mandate). The absence of enforceability makes the other three links operationally unreliable, even when they are otherwise strong.

The analysis applies a weak-link rule: if any single link is absent, the chain is classified as broken, regardless of the strength of the other links. A provision with a strong norm and a designated actor but no explicit authority is classified as partially covered, not as compliant. This is consistent with the administrative law principle that capacity without authority is not a lawful basis for public action.

## Corpus discovery

A central methodological commitment of NormTrace-IHR is that the normative corpus is discovered through a systematic search rather than assumed on the basis of the analyst's prior knowledge. This commitment addresses a recurring limitation in IHR implementation assessments: when analysts begin with a predefined list of "relevant laws," they tend to reproduce conventional assumptions about which laws matter (health legislation) and overlook sectoral instruments (aviation law, customs law, animal health legislation, data protection law) that may contain -- or contradict -- critical IHR provisions.

The corpus discovery phase queries eight sectors: health and epidemic disease, migration and border control, customs and trade, civil aviation, maritime navigation and ports, animal and plant health, civil protection and emergency management, and personal data protection. For each sector, multiple query formulations are used to maximise recall across different legislative naming conventions. A direct search of the country's official legislative repository is also conducted.

The result of this phase is a classified inventory, not a pre-selected corpus. Each instrument found is assigned to one of four categories:

*Include* -- direct intersection with at least one IHR provision in the selected set, confirmed on the basis of the instrument's stated scope or accessible text.

*Review* -- possible relevance that cannot be confirmed without text examination. This category exists to prevent premature exclusion of instruments whose scope is uncertain from their title or summary alone.

*Discard* -- no intersection with any selected IHR provision. Discards are documented rather than silently excluded, because the explicit negative finding -- "this instrument was examined and found not applicable" -- has methodological value. The category includes instruments that have a title suggesting relevance but a subject matter that does not engage any of the six selection tests. These are recorded as cases of "apparent coverage."

*Laguna* -- the instrument is known to exist (through reference in other instruments, legal databases, or secondary sources) but its text could not be obtained. Lagunas are documented as gaps in the corpus rather than treated as absences of the instrument.

The user validates this classified inventory before analysis begins. The user may add instruments not found in the automated search -- this is the primary mechanism for incorporating the analyst's domain knowledge of a specific legal system -- reclassify items, and annotate reform status where recent amendments are known that have not yet been captured in official repositories.

## Analysis across the corpus

Once the corpus is validated, the analysis proceeds provision by provision, but instrument by instrument within each provision. The analysis does not stop at the first instrument that appears to provide coverage; it examines all instruments in the corpus to identify dispersed coverage, apparent coverage, complementarity, and contradiction.

Dispersed coverage -- where the four links are individually present across multiple instruments but there is no legal coordinator activating them in concert -- is the most common finding in federal systems and in states with fragmented sectoral legislation. It is treated as a distinct finding rather than as full compliance, because the practical effect of dispersed coverage in a PHEIC scenario is that no single authority has a clear mandate to coordinate the response across institutional hierarchies, and each sector may act (or fail to act) according to its own chain of command.

Contradiction -- where one norm in the corpus prevents what another enables -- is relatively uncommon but analytically important. The most frequent form is a conflict between a data-sharing obligation under the IHR (Arts. 6, 7, 10) and a data protection law that prohibits sharing identifiable health information with international organisations without an explicit domestic statutory exception. This is not a gap in the health law framework; it is a conflict between two valid domestic norms, and resolving it requires amendment of the data protection law rather than of health legislation.

## Source traceability and currency

Every finding in the analysis is required to cite the specific instrument, the article or provision number within that instrument, the source URL or file reference, and the date on which the text was consulted. The analysis also records, where identifiable, the date of the instrument's most recent amendment -- expressed in the format used by the relevant official gazette (for example, "DOF 15-01-2026" for the Mexican Diario Oficial de la Federacion).

These dates are stored alongside the analysis results in the database and are displayed to users when they consult a prior analysis. This allows a user to assess, at a glance, whether any instrument in the corpus has been amended since the analysis was conducted and to decide whether a new analysis is warranted. The tool does not automate this decision: determining whether a reform is substantively relevant to the IHR provisions covered by the analysis requires judgement that cannot be delegated to a classification algorithm.

## The C1 normative score and its relationship to the e-SPAR

The e-SPAR (State Party Self-Assessment Annual Reporting) assigns scores on a scale of 1 to 5 to five indicators under IHR Core Capacity 1: legislative adequacy (C1.1), financing (C1.2), intersectoral coordination (C1.3), preparedness and PHEIC response (C1.4), and accountability and reporting (C1.5). The e-SPAR reference used in any NormTrace-IHR analysis is retrieved from the WHO extranet at `https://extranet.who.int/e-spar` and the consultation date is recorded alongside the score.

The normative C1 score produced by NormTrace-IHR uses the same 1-to-5 scale, with descriptors calibrated to correspond to recognisable legislative situations:

Score 5 applies where the corpus contains an express norm of appropriate rank, a formally designated actor with a specific IHR mandate, authority explicitly conferred for the required actions, and a functioning enforceability mechanism, with preparedness financing mandated rather than discretionary.

Score 4 applies where most of these elements are present but enforceability is partial, financing is contingent on annual budget decisions, or intersectoral coordination rests on a published protocol rather than a statute.

Score 3 applies where the legal basis exists in general terms -- a health ministry with general epidemic disease powers, for example -- but no specific IHR mandate is enumerated, intersectoral coordination is administrative rather than statutory, and the connection between the general mandate and specific IHR functions requires interpretive inference.

Score 2 applies where the relevant legislation predates the IHR 2005 and has not been updated to reflect the revised obligations, or where partial sectoral legislation exists but significant provisions are uncovered.

Score 1 applies where the only domestic legal basis for IHR compliance is the promulgation decree of the treaty itself, with no domestic institutional infrastructure enabling its exercise.

The weak-link rule applies to the aggregate score: if any single indicator scores 1, the weighted aggregate cannot exceed 2.5.

The difference between the e-SPAR self-reported score and the NormTrace-IHR normative score is expressed as a signed delta. A positive delta -- where the self-reported score exceeds the normative score -- is described as normative over-reporting. This is not assumed to reflect deliberate misrepresentation; it most commonly reflects genuine uncertainty about whether existing general-purpose instruments satisfy the more specific IHR requirements, or a conflation of operational practice with legal authority. A negative delta -- where the normative score exceeds the self-reported score -- reflects normative under-reporting, a pattern that may indicate incomplete awareness of how existing sectoral instruments interact to cover IHR obligations.

## Relationship to the JEE and other assessment instruments

The Joint External Evaluation (JEE) is a voluntary, peer-reviewed assessment of IHR capacity that is methodologically more rigorous than the e-SPAR because it involves external experts, field visits, and operational verification. Where a JEE exists for a country under analysis, the following triangulation logic applies:

If the JEE identifies a gap and the normative analysis also identifies it, the finding is doubly supported from independent methodologies and should be treated as high-confidence.

If the JEE does not identify a gap that the normative analysis does, the gap likely exists at the legal level but is compensated by operational practice. This is a fragile situation: the operational compensation may be effective during normal conditions but unavailable during the high-stress early phase of a PHEIC, when normal institutional relationships break down and legal authority becomes the fallback.

If the JEE identifies a gap that the normative analysis does not, the gap is likely technical or operational rather than normative -- for example, laboratory equipment, trained personnel, or logistics. This type of gap is outside the scope of NormTrace-IHR.

## Transparency regarding automated analysis

The block-by-block normative analysis in NormTrace-IHR is conducted with the assistance of a large language model (Anthropic claude-sonnet-4). The model does not apply independent legal judgement; it applies the analytical framework described in this document, as encoded in the structured prompts in `backend/skill_prompt.py`. The framework determines which provisions are analysed, what tests are applied, and what categories of finding are available. The model's role is to apply that framework to the specific instruments in the validated corpus and to render findings in a structured format.

Users should be aware of two limitations that follow from this architecture. First, the model's identification of relevant provisions within a legal text depends on its ability to read and interpret legal language, which may vary in quality across languages and legal traditions. Instruments in less-resourced languages may receive less accurate coverage identification than instruments in English, Spanish, or French. Second, the model applies the analytical framework consistently but cannot detect what is not in the corpus: if an instrument that would resolve a gap is not in the validated corpus, the gap will be reported even if the instrument exists. The user validation step at the corpus stage is the primary safeguard against this.

All analytical prompts are version-controlled in the repository. Changes to the prompts that alter the analytical framework should be accompanied by an update to this document and a new version number.
